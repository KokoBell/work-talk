***a look at the tools central banks use to conduct monetary policy***

On the 27th of January 2022, the South African Reserve Bank increased the repurchase rate (otherwise known as the “repo rate”) by 0.25 percentage points to 4% per year. Weeks before the first lockdown restrictions were put in place in 2020, the Reserve Bank had lowered that interest rate by 1 percentage point to 5.25% per year, with further decreases later in that year. The decline in the repo rate over 2020-21 was meant to revive the economy by making borrowing cheaper. With a couple of [central banks around the world now increasing interest rates amid fears of inflation](https://www.ft.com/content/f76d5033-ed8f-46fa-9313-e93597cee06f), we explore how Central Banks’ actions at the early stages of the covid-19 global pandemic were supposed to lower interest rates charged by commercial banks on borrowers, in hopes of encouraging spending and investment in the then rapidly-sinking economy.

***The interest rate is the price of borrowing***

The interest rate, for borrowers, is the cost of borrowing money as it is a portion of money - over and above the amount that was initially borrowed – that they must repay by the time repayment of a loan is due. With high interest rates, it becomes more costly to borrow, and it is thought that people become less willing to take on debt, and consequently, economy-wide borrowing decreases, which if uncompensated for by higher income, generally leads to lower expenditure in the economy. Thus, by lowering the cost of borrowing (interest rate), the central bank hopes that borrowers (typically businesses and people) will borrow more and will therefore have a greater ability to spend. Spending can of course be a boost for the economy as it can encourage greater production of goods and services, which at times may require greater employment. But people and business do not borrow from central banks, they borrow from commercial banks (such as ABSA; Standard Bank; and Capitec) and other lending institutions. Luckily, central banks have some degree of authority over commercial banks and are able to affect what interest rates commercial banks charge when they lend out money to their customers.

***Commercial banks can be influenced by central bank regulations and activities***

Commercial banks, like other businesses, are primarily motivated by profit. One of the main costs of a commercial bank are the interest rates it has promised to customers that deposit their money into accounts held at the bank. While one of their main sources of revenue is the interest rate that banks charge their customers when lending them money. Thus, at least traditionally, the primary way a commercial bank makes profit is the difference in the interest rates they charge and the interest rates they promise (known as the interest spread). Commercial banks lend money to entities such as businesses, people, and to other commercial banks. In order to protect the integrity of the financial system even in times of panic, for example where many depositors want to withdraw at once, banks have a daily obligated to keep some portion of the money they hold at the central bank (this money is known as reserves) and to keep a fixed proportion of the depositor’s money as cash in hand. Where commercial banks are unable, by themselves, to meet those obligation, they can borrow money from other commercial banks and from the central bank, each of which charges their own interest rate on the loan. If due to excessive lending or other money-making activities, commercial banks fall short of the funds needed to meet their obligations they will have to borrow. If, however, the borrowing costs are too high, the profit-motive of the commercial banks dictates that they either slow down their lending and other money-making activities so that they can meet their obligations, or that the commercial banks now charge higher interest rates when lending to their customers, in order to compensate for their increased borrowing costs. Note that commercial banks in many instances defer to central banks – either to borrow money or to fulfil their reserve requirements. This is the source of the central bank’s power and what allows them to influence interest rates, the extent to which people and businesses borrow.

***Central banks can use monetary policy tools to change commercial banks’ interest rates***

The central bank, in normal times (i.e., outside events such as the 2020-21 Coronavirus Pandemic or the 2007-08 Global Financial Crisis), uses three tools to try and get commercial banks to charge lower interest rates. First, we have **Open Market Purchases**, where the central bank buys up Treasury bills, which are short-term interest-earning assets held by commercial banks, thus injecting them with cash (which of course earns no interest). Commercial banks then face the choice of what to do with this extra cash. With commercial banks having more cash, they each face less stress to meet their obligations. This will prompt commercial banks to have less demand to borrow from each other, resulting in there being lower cost for banks to lend to each other. With the commercial banks’ borrowing costs lower, there is less pressure on the interest rate they charge customers when lending out money. This can lead to a lower interest rate charged on borrowers (business and people), which may go even lower if the commercial banks try to attract more customers and thus compete with other commercial banks by charging borrowers more attractive (lower) interest rates on loans.

Secondly, we have the complicated **Discount Policy**. This is the policy the Reserve Bank employed on the 27th of January 2022 and for most of 2020-21 in response to the economic downturn created by the covid-19 pandemic. It is where the central bank charges a lower interest rate towards commercial banks that need to borrow funds to fulfil their reserve requirements. Because the central bank competes with other commercial bank with regard to lending to commercial banks that need to meet their obligations, the interest charged by the central bank cannot be excessively higher than that charged by commercial banks towards each other. Additionally, the reserves made available by commercial banks to other commercial banks for the sake of borrowing to fulfil their reserve requirement, must be less than what is required for the deficient commercial banks to meet that obligation. This is to ensure that indeed the commercial banks (as lenders) compete with the central bank, otherwise the commercial banks would just continue lending to each other at the initial rate. When the central bank and commercial banks compete in that reserve market, a lower interest rate charged by the central bank will force the commercial banks to also lower the interest rate they charge one another, or else commercial banks would just borrow all their funds they need for obligations from the central bank. And as in the first instance, the lower borrowing costs may lead to lower interest rate charged by commercial banks toward their customers (businesses and people).

Lastly, we have **Reserve Requirement policy**, wherein the central bank allows commercial banks to store a lower percentage of their customer’s deposits as reserves. This frees up a lot the commercial banks’ cash holding, thus putting them under less pressure to borrow from other banks or the reserve bank in order to meet their obligations. This lower demand to borrow from other banks will lead to a drop in the interbank rate, and so with lower borrowing costs, commercial banks may charge a lower interest rate on prospective borrowers.

The three monetary policy tools we have just explored fall under what are termed “conventional monetary tools” and only constitute a portion of the instruments central banks all over the world use to try and affect interest rates. These tools are often insufficient in times of financial panic, requiring the central bank to deploy tools such as ‘liquidity provisions’ and ‘asset purchases’, which we can touch on at a later stage.
