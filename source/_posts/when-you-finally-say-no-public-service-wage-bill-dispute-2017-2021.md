This article is a summary of the wage dispute between the South African government and public service workers, which occurred between 2017 and 2021. The wage dispute originated from government and the public service workers reaching an agreement despite the government having little willingness and, apparently, little capability of meeting the terms of the agreement. It eventually culminated in the government flat out refusing to carry out the last phase of the agreement, with the public servants employing different means including Courts and mass action to try to force the government’s hand to fully implement the agreement. The wage dispute came to an end in mid-2021, with unions, reluctantly agreeing to the government’s new proposals. This article has four objectives: 1) to understand why the dispute started; 2) to uncover what factors led it to persist for as long as it did; 3) to detail what moves each party used to try and tilt the situation into their favor, and 4) how the dispute came to be resolved.

To understand why the dispute started, we look at terms agreed upon in the 2018 wage agreement and the degree to which those terms satisfied each party’s initial demands. Coming into 2017 wage negotiations, the government was already complaining about how the 2015 wage agreement put pressure on public finances such that it had to limit the public service headcount just to afford the wage bill and had to also devote less money to capital expenditure and service delivery. It therefore hoped that unlike in previous terms, the wage growth implied by the next agreement does not outpace consumer inflation rates. To that end, the government suggested abandoning ‘Occupation Specific Dispensation’ - which had allowed occupations such as educators to earn significantly more than they would have by virtue of their occupation – and performance bonuses (for the 3 years after 2017), while also being critical of pay progression (fixed annual changes to one’s salary). Public Servants on the other hand asked for wage negotiations to be done on an annual basis, as longer-term deals typically paid less in their latter years, with that fact being particularly pressing considering the uncertainty caused by the then recent junk bond rating of South Africa’s debt. Furthermore, workers called for a scrapping of the level 1-3 salary grades, as well as a faster and across the board pay progression. Wage increase demands were as follows: 12% for level 4-7; with 11% for levels 8-10; and 10% for levels 11-12. The unions viewed these above-inflation demands as reasonable in light of worsened conditions such as the higher medical aid costs they were faced with. Following negotiations held at the Public Sector Coordinating Bargaining Council, on the 8th of June 2018, a new deal between government and labor was struck. This deal spanned 3 periods (2018/19 to 2020/21), with salary increases for the levels 1-10 ranging from CPI + 0.5% to CPI + 1.5%, while levels 11-12 were promised an above-inflation wage increase for the 2018/19 period only. Pay progression was to be done across the board at 1.5% annually. Further terms included more expansive housing allowance. The 2018 deal featured concessions on the part of both parties, with pay progression maintained, expanded, and annualized, just as labor hoped but against the wishes of government. Wage increases were only slightly above expected consumer inflation; thus, they were just beyond the government’s target but a far cry from what labor had hoped for, assuming that the latter’s initial demands was not simply a ploy to maintain bargaining space. Labour further lost out as the deal spanned more than a single financial year. Overall, considering that both parties made concessions, and that neither could say a term that they were adamant about was extremely neglected or violated, it is unlikely that either party walked away from the wage negotiation and agreement feeling hard done by. As to the feasibility of fully carrying out the 2018 wage agreement, National Treasury said that over the duration of the deal, spending implied by the wage agreement exceeded planned spending on salary increases and other conditions of service by about R30.2 billion. Treasury however said they would not provide any new money and that the R30.2 billion shortfall would have to be covered by departments’ adjustment of compensation baselines. In other words, departments would have to use money that it had reserved for other items, in order to afford the salary increases for its personnel. At that point, while unaffordable, the wage bill did not appear unfeasible, with Treasury even promising to send through the Department of Public Service and Administration to assist departments that are facing increased wage cost pressures.

From 2018 through to early 2020, the terms of the 2018 agreement were carried out seamlessly. So, when did problems with implementing the deal begin? Despite the deal being put into effect until early 2020, throughout 2019 there had been grumblings from the Treasury and the then Minister of Finance, Tito Mboweni, on the burden the wage bill had been having on public finances. They noted that low economic growth, tax revenue shortfalls and assistance of State-owned entities in financial crisis, have necessitated expenditure adjustments including a reduction on the money available to departments for compensation spending by about R27 billion. Other proposals to slow the growth of the wage bill over the medium term were the abolition of performance bonuses (in favor of performance management measures); allowing early retirement of public servants; pegging cost-of-living adjustments at or below consumer inflation; and reviewing ‘Occupation Specific Dispensation’ and automatic pay progression. Furthermore, they claimed that compensation spending had been eating up on items such as textbooks and dams, and that money should be reallocated from compensation to capital expenditure. In late 2019, the COSATU-affiliated SADTU expressed anger at the then Deputy Minister of Finance’s utterances at an investment conference, who apparently said cutting the public service wage bill would be the most practical way of recovering the state of public finances. Through early 2020, the finance minister (Mboweni) and the Treasury became more concrete in their stance, proposing a R160 billion cut in the wage bill over the medium term, with the spread in reductions as follows: R37.8 billion for 2020/21; R55 Billion for 2021/22; and R67.5 billion for 2022/23. The reductions implied that the last phase of the 2018 agreement would not be carried out fully. Additionally, these wage bill reductions were part of the Treasury’s target of achieving a primary budget balance by 2022/23. Treasury claimed that the R160 billion in reductions would come from changes to cost-of-living adjustments, pay progression, and other benefits. They said they were already in communication with labor – through the PSCBC – on how the reductions may be accomplished. Labour unions confirmed that on the 25th of February, the government - through the PSCBC – indeed proposed a review of the 2018 wage agreement and tabled an offer of 0% salary increases from April 2020 onwards. Unions including COSATU (and its affiliates) strongly rejected the call to review (and consequently renege on) the 2018 wage agreement, with NEHAWU even threatening national mass action on the 30th of March if indeed the government planned to neglect the 2018 deal. On the 31st of March 2020, the Department of Public Service and Administration issued a statement wherein the then Minister Sizwe Mchunu reassured workers that despite the stress on public finances – not helped by the outbreak of the coronavirus and the downgrading of South Africa’s government bonds – the government remained committed to implementing the 2018 wage agreement.

Despite the wage agreement and the government’s promise just a day earlier, the 1st of April came and went with no salary increases in sight for public servants. On the 15th of the same month, NEHAWU expressed anger over the government’s no-show on the 1st, and again vowed to mobilize workers for mass action, in addition to filling for Conciliation – via the PSCBC – to force the government to fully implement their end of the deal. At the Conciliation, set for the 28th to the 30th of April, NEHAWU would be joined by other COSATU affiliated unions including SADTU and DENOSA. The Conciliation process ended with no resolution, prompting the unions to pursue arbitration. In the Supplementary budget in mid-2020 – which was given following expenditure changes induced by the lockdown because of the coronavirus outbreak – the government claimed that the cutting down of the wage bill had not yet been finalized. Regardless, NEHAWU said it had been incensed by the continued non-implementation of the deal, and especially by claims apparently made by leadership at the Department of Public Service and Administration that reneging on the deal was necessary otherwise the government would have sunk R37.8 billion further into debt. NEHAWU, and COSATU a month later, then called on a national strike to be held on the 7th of October, protesting government’s refusal to carry out the last phase of the 2018 wage agreement. Treasury offered more clarity in its Medium-Term Budget Policy statement, saying that it had abandoned the 2018 wage agreement because it wanted to reduce the wage bill in the medium term. Compensation spending was designated to grow at 0.8% over the next three financial periods, while Treasury was to also explore reconsidering pay progression and ‘Occupation Specific Dispensation’. Again, reduction in the wage bill was framed as part of the long-term strategy of reducing debt-to-GDP and moving expenditure away from consumption and towards investment. Thus, the government had bypassed the PSCBC – the institution under which collective bargaining had undergone – and unilaterally decided upon the trajectory of public servants’ wages, much to the ire of public servants.

The arbitration process launched at the PSCBC was however superseded by the Labour Appeals Court case, with the unions – headed by the Public Service Association – taking on government, the former who wanted to compel the latter to implement the last leg of the deal, while the government countered, questioning the legality of the 2018 agreement in the first instance. The unions argued that the agreement had been approved by the appropriate authority (by the Minister of Finance, and thus by implication, the Cabinet, in which the Minister is a member), and that there had been recognition and planning for the R30.2 billion shortfall in planned compensation expenditure. The government refuted that argument, saying that Treasury – the appropriate authority – and not the finance minister, had made no commitment to fund the shortfall or to make other departments make up for the shortfall. Further, the government argued that the invalidity of the agreement originated from the fact that Regulation 79 of the Public Service Act of 1994 – stating that the cost of a bargaining agreement must be covered by the budget of the relevant department, otherwise there must be a commitment from the Treasury – had not been satisfied. Ultimately, the court found that it would not be just and equitable for the government to “expend significant and scarce financial resources” on public servants in the context of the need to recover an economy which millions of vulnerable people depend on. Furthermore, the enforcement of the government’s promise to carry out the salary increments was declared illegal as it contravened the Constitution (1996) and the Public Service Regulations (2016). NEHAWU and COSATU unions responded by planning a sit in at the Union Buildings in early December 2020. In February 2021, SADTU; POPCRU; DENOSA and later on, NEHAWU, formally protested the Labour Appeals Court decision by lodging an appeal at the Constitutional Court, claiming, among other things that the Labour Appeals Court failed to consider that the issue of invalidity of the agreement had previously not arose, that it excuses the government from being obligated to carry out a bargaining agreement, and that it offends the Labour Relations Act as well as the Constitution.

In their presentation for the budget in 2021, Treasury showed appreciation for the Labour Appeals Court decision, and announced that in line with its plans to decelerate the growth of wage bill, compensation spending would grow by 1.2% over the medium term. That target was made prior to the conclusion of the latest wage agreement, illustrating the government’s commitment to contain compensation spending, whatever the public servants’ demands may have been. Treasury said reducing the wage bill would entail carrying out the measures it had set out in previous statements. COSATU threw its support to the unions awaiting the Constitutional Court’s decision, and further vowed to conduct mass action on the 7th of October 2021. Meanwhile, from March onwards workers and government convened at the PSCBC, with workers demanding CPI + 4% increases as well as a “special risk allowance” amounting to 12% of worker’s basic salaries, in light of the coronavirus pandemic. Predictably, government tabled a 0% salary increase offer as well as the scraping of some other allowances, an offer which was promptly rejected by unions, leading to breakdowns in negotiations by May. Following a facilitation process, government revised their offer and tabled an across-the-board cash allowance of over R900, and 1.5% pensionable pay progression. This again was rejected by POPCRU and NEHAWU who instead sought a dispute resolution. Nevertheless, enough unions including PSA, SADTU and DENOSA accepted government’s latest offer, meaning it would also be binding for those in opposition. The 2021 wage agreement is slated for a period of 1 year but will remain effective if no new deal is concluded in 2022. It is composed of a non-pensionable cash allowance ranging from R1220 per month for level 1 workers and R1695 for level 12 workers – backdated to April 2021 – as well as a pensionable 1.5% increase to public servants that otherwise would not have qualified for pay progression.

The upcoming Constitutional Court judgement will signal the official end of the dispute. Looking back however, it seems almost inevitable that the agreement between public servants and government was going to turn fall apart. For the few years preceding the signing of the 2018 agreement, government was gradually coming to the realization that its expenditure in the economy was not producing sufficient economic growth – and consequently tax revenue – to justify the spending allocation to some expenditure items. This can be inferred from the expenditure ceilings the Treasury adopted following periods of low growth. More importantly, as seen from its projections, the government did not anticipate that the economy would expand sufficiently (over 3%) over the medium term. There was also the problem of mounting government debt, which was leading to more and more tax revenue being eaten away by interest spending. Thus, a wage agreement where salaries would increase at or above inflation over the medium term – considering expenditure ceilings – could only be carried out if the government either borrowed more, or it made cuts to some expenditure items. Early proposals leaned towards the latter option, where there would be reallocation of money within departments but following Tito Mboweni’s arrival in 2019, along with growing spending commitments (such as Eskom), Treasury showed little tolerance for the “unsustainable” public sector wage bill and immediately planned to cut it in favor of capital expenditure and the reduction of public debt. So, by 2019, the government had already taken official steps – via the PSCBC – in its quest to not carry out the last phase of the 2018 agreement. Those steps were further concretized by the government’s commitment to a primary budget balance by 2023/24, which implied further cuts in the wage bill for the medium term. Thus, before any individual contracted covid-19 in South Africa, the government had made strides in ensuring that the 2018 deal will not be carried out fully, and that the subsequent 2021 wage agreement would feature a severely downgraded wage package than one public servants had (rightly or wrongly) grown accustomed to.

In that context, it is hard to escape the feeling that the financial crisis – resulting in increased government borrowing and spending - brought on by the coronavirus outbreak operated merely as a convenient excuse for what the government was going to do anyway. Moreover, that the government finally bailed on the 2018 agreement amid the pandemic was important for two reasons. First it meant that public servants (who are some of the most comfortably paid workers in the country) would probably find little support from the public (many of whom were lowly paid or had lost jobs). Secondly, in its Labour Appeals Court arguments, it allowed the government to successfully use the urgency of the health, job, and income crises as one of the main reasons why it cannot follow through on the agreement. It was therefore highly beneficial to the government that the wage dispute played out in the background of covid-19, as it enabled them to shrewdly offload the burden of the 2018 agreement and carry out the planned slow-down to public service compensation, which it had been quietly mulling over for the better part of a decade.
